# Sales Page

Write a complete long-form sales page with strong structure, clear mechanism, believable proof handling, and conversion-focused flow.

## Inputs

Collect these before writing:
- product or offer name
- price point
- core mechanism or core benefit
- target audience

Ask follow-up questions if the brief is too thin to write responsibly.

Helpful optional inputs:
- proof points
- testimonials
- guarantee details
- bonuses
- urgency mechanism
- traffic temperature
- differentiators

## Output Target

By default, produce a substantial long-form page.

Guidance:
- aim for depth, not fluff
- use sections, subheads, and rhythm changes
- do not force a minimum word count if the available facts do not support it
- prefer completeness and credibility over empty expansion

## Workflow

### 1) Frame the sale

Establish:
- what is being sold
- who it is for
- what outcome it promises
- what mechanism makes the outcome plausible
- why this offer matters now

Keep the page grounded in a specific transformation.

### 2) Build tangible outcome anchors

Do not sell generic speed or vague success.

Anchor benefits to concrete outcomes when supported by the brief, such as:
- launching more offers in less time
- validating ideas earlier
- reducing wasted effort
- publishing faster with structure
- converting attention into a specific business asset

If the user did not provide measurable outcomes, use cautious, non-fabricated language.

### 3) Handle proof responsibly

Proof can include:
- actual results provided by the user
- real case studies
- timelines
- process breakdowns
- demonstrations
- logic-based validation
- screenshots only if they actually exist

Rules:
- do not invent testimonials
- do not invent quantified revenue or conversion results
- do not imply screenshots or social proof exist if they were not provided
- if proof is limited, use honest logical validation instead of fake authority

### 4) Address objections

Pre-handle the most relevant objections for the offer.

Common categories:
- time
- technical ability
- skepticism
- prior bad purchases
- lack of audience
- lack of clarity
- payment or setup fear
- uncertainty about fit

Prefer 5-10 strong objections over filler.

### 5) Build the identity layer

Show not just what the buyer gets, but who they become.

Possible identity angles:
- operator
- builder
- disciplined creator
- early mover
- business owner with systems
- confident implementer

Keep the identity shift believable and connected to the actual offer.

### 6) Add future visualization

Include one or two vivid moments that help the buyer imagine the post-purchase reality.

Examples:
- seeing the finished asset live
- launching sooner than expected
- opening a dashboard and seeing something finally working
- publishing without the usual stall cycle

Make it concrete, not melodramatic.

### 7) Differentiate the offer

Explain what this is not.

Examples:
- not just a prompt pack
- not another generic course
- not empty AI hype
- not a confusing all-in-one system without implementation support

Then explain what makes this offer distinct.

### 8) Use urgency honestly

If a real urgency mechanism exists, use it:
- founder pricing
- deadline
- bonus expiry
- limited seats
- limited rollout

Do not manufacture fake scarcity.

### 9) Close with a CTA stack

End with:
- what they get
- what happens immediately after purchase
- guarantee or risk-reduction only if real
- urgency reminder if real
- final identity or outcome hook

Make the next step obvious.

## Recommended Structure

Adapt as needed, but this is the default:

1. headline
2. opening hook or scenario
3. problem expansion
4. mechanism explanation
5. outcome framing
6. proof or logical validation
7. what they get
8. differentiation
9. objection handling
10. future pacing / visualization
11. urgency
12. risk reversal
13. CTA stack

## Tone

Aim for:
- confident
- calm authority
- specific
- persuasive
- not gimmicky
- not bloated
- not bro-marketing

If the brand voice is sharper or more aggressive, keep it controlled and credible.

## Output Rules

- output polished final copy unless the user asks for outlines, variants, or analysis
- do not show hidden reasoning
- do not fabricate proof, testimonials, screenshots, metrics, guarantees, deadlines, or customer outcomes
- when facts are thin, reduce certainty rather than inventing support
- if legal, compliance, or earnings-risk concerns apply, use grounded claims and avoid exaggerated promises
