## VIDEO 1.2: WHY MOST PROMPTS FAIL (10 min)

### SCRIPT

"Let me show you why most people struggle with AI.

You open ChatGPT. You type: 'Write me a marketing email.'

And you get something like: 'Introducing our new product. It helps you achieve your goals. Sign up today to get started...'

Generic. Boring. Completely unusable.

So you try again. 'Write a better marketing email.'

And you get: 'Are you tired of struggling with marketing? Our revolutionary product will transform your business...'

Still terrible. Still generic. Still sounds like every other AI-generated email on the internet.

Here's what's happening: You're asking AI to read your mind. And AI can't read minds.

When you say 'Write a marketing email,' you're giving AI zero context about:
- Who your audience is
- What problem they have
- What your product actually does
- What tone you want
- What format you need

So AI defaults to the most generic, safest, most average version of a marketing email.

It's not AI's fault. It's not that AI is bad. It's that you're not giving it enough information to work with.

Think of it like this: If you hired a copywriter and said 'Write me an email,' then gave them no brief, no audience info, no product details—what would you expect?

You'd expect generic garbage. Because they don't have enough information to write something good.

AI is the same way. Garbage in, garbage out.

The difference between marketers who get generic output and marketers who get exceptional output isn't the AI tool they're using. It's how they're using it.

And that's what this course is about."

## VIDEO 1.3: THE PSYCHOLOGY OF EFFECTIVE PROMPTING (10 min)

### SCRIPT

"To understand how to prompt effectively, you need to understand how AI thinks.

AI doesn't think like humans. It doesn't have context about your business, your audience, or your goals unless you give it that context.

Here's what AI is actually doing when you give it a prompt:

It's predicting what words should come next based on patterns it learned from training data.

When you say 'Write a marketing email,' AI thinks: 'What words typically follow this request in the training data?'

And the training data has millions of generic, mediocre marketing emails. So that's what it gives you.

But when you give AI specific context—when you tell it exactly who it's writing for, what problem they're facing, what tone to use—now it has a specific pattern to match.

Think of AI like a really smart, really fast intern who knows a lot about everything but knows nothing about your specific situation.

Your job as the prompt engineer is to give that intern a complete brief.

Here's what a complete brief includes:

One: Role. Who should AI pretend to be? An expert copywriter? A conversion specialist? A content strategist?

Two: Context. What's the situation? Who's the audience? What do they need?

Three: Task. What exactly should AI create? What format? What length?

Four: Constraints. What should it avoid? What must it include?

Five: Examples. What does good output look like?

When you give AI all five of these, you get completely different results.

Let me show you...

Bad prompt: 'Write a marketing email'

Better prompt: 'You are a conversion copywriter with 10 years of experience writing emails for SaaS companies. Write a welcome email for new users of a project management tool aimed at marketing agencies. The email should thank them, set expectations, and get them to take their first action. Tone: professional but friendly. Length: 200 words.'

See the difference? The second prompt gives AI a role, context, task, and constraints.

And the output is completely different.

This is the foundation of everything we're going to cover in this course."

## VIDEO 2.1: FRAMEWORK 1 - ROLE-CONTEXT-TASK (8 min)

### SCRIPT

"Framework 1 is Role-Context-Task. This is the foundation that makes everything else work.

Here's the formula:

Role: You are a [specific expert] with [credentials]
Context: We're creating [content] for [audience] who [situation]
Task: Write [output] with [requirements]

Let me break down each part.

Role: This tells AI who to pretend to be. Not just 'a writer' but 'a conversion copywriter with 10 years of SaaS experience.' The more specific, the better.

Context: This gives AI the situation. Who is this for? What do they need? What's their pain point?

Task: This is the specific output you want. What format? What length? What must it include?

## VIDEO 2.2: FRAMEWORK 2 - STRATEGIC CONSTRAINT (7 min)

### SCRIPT

"Framework 2 is the Strategic Constraint Method.

This seems counterintuitive, but adding limitations actually increases creativity.

When you tell AI 'Write a headline,' it defaults to the most common, generic headlines.

But when you say 'Write a headline without using the words: best, amazing, ultimate, or perfect'—now AI has to think differently.

Constraints force creativity.

Common constraints that work:
- Word restrictions (don't use X, Y, Z)
- Length constraints (exactly 10 words)
- Tone constraints (must sound casual, not corporate)
- Format constraints (must be a question)
- Angle constraints (approach from skeptic's perspective)

## VIDEO 2.3: FRAMEWORK 3 - CHAIN-OF-THOUGHT (7 min)

### SCRIPT

"Framework 3 is Chain-of-Thought Sequencing.

Use this for complex tasks that have multiple parts.

Instead of asking for a 5-email sequence all at once—which gives you disjointed, repetitive emails—break it into steps.

Step 1: Write Email 1
Step 2: Write Email 2 that references Email 1
Step 3: Write Email 3 that builds on the story
And so on...

Each step builds on the previous one, creating a cohesive sequence.

This works for:
- Email sequences
- Sales pages (section by section)
- Course outlines
- Multi-part content series

The key is telling AI to connect each step to the previous one."
